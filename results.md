## Research Results

Tested 5 water sources across Ho Chi Minh City, March 2026.  
All TDS values normalized to 25°C using ISO 7888 temperature compensation.  
Standard reference: Vietnam QCVN 01-1:2024/BYT (TDS limit: 1000 ppm, temp limit: 30°C)

| Source | Raw TDS (ppm) | Temp (°C) | TDS @25°C (ppm) | QCVN Status | Drinkable? |
|--------|--------------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|------------|
| Tap water | 103 | 28.4 | 103 | ✓ Pass | ✓ Yes |
| Home RO filter | 105 | 27.9 | 105 | ✓ Pass | ✓ Yes |
| Aquafina bottled | 1 | 25.0 | 1 | ✓ Pass | ✓ Yes |
| Saltwater (control test) | 1081 | 31.2 | 983 | ✓ Pass* | ✗ No |
| Natural pond water | 171 | 29.1 | 171 | ✓ Pass | ⚠️ Not recommended |

*Note on saltwater: TDS 983 ppm technically passes the QCVN 1000 ppm limit, 
but saltwater is obviously not drinkable. This highlights that TDS alone 
is not a complete measure of water safety — it does not detect biological 
contaminants, heavy metals, or specific ions like sodium. 
QCVN compliance ≠ drinkable in all cases.

---

### Key Finding

The home RO filter (105 ppm) produced water nearly identical to 
unfiltered tap water (103 ppm). A properly functioning RO filter 
typically reduces TDS by 90–99%, so we would expect output 
below ~15 ppm if the filter were working correctly. 
A reading of 105 ppm indicates significant membrane degradation.

### Effect of Temperature Compensation

Without ISO 7888 compensation, the saltwater sample read 1081 ppm.  
After compensation to 25°C, the corrected value was 983 ppm — 
a difference of **98 ppm (9.1%)**.  
This demonstrates why temperature compensation is essential 
for any meaningful cross-sample comparison.
